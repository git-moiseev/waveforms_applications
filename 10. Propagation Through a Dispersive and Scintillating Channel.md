# 10. Propagation Through a Dispersive and Scintillating Channel

The proposed waveform is intended for interstellar transmission and must therefore remain detectable after propagation through the ionized interstellar medium (ISM). At microwave frequencies, the dominant propagation effects are plasma dispersion, multipath scattering, phase fluctuations, and interstellar scintillation.

For typical lines of sight within the nearby Galactic neighborhood (approximately 1000 light-years or 300 pc), these effects are not expected to alter the fundamental frequency geometry of the proposed waveform. Instead, they primarily influence the received amplitude, phase coherence, and detection probability.

## 10.1 Linear Propagation Model

The received signal may be represented as

$$
r(t,f)=h(t,f)s(t,f)+n(t,f),
$$

where

- $s(t,f)$ is the transmitted waveform,
- $h(t,f)$ is the complex propagation channel,
- $n(t,f)$ is receiver noise.

The interstellar medium behaves as a nearly linear propagation channel at radio frequencies. It neither generates new spectral components nor converts one transmitted carrier into another. Instead, it introduces frequency-dependent delay together with slowly varying amplitude and phase.

Consequently, the transmitted two-frequency structure remains a two-frequency structure after propagation.

---

## 10.2 Plasma Dispersion

Propagation through ionized plasma produces a frequency-dependent group delay

$$
t_{\mathrm{DM}}=4.15\,
\mathrm{ms}\,
DM\,
\nu_{\mathrm{GHz}}^{-2},
$$

where $DM$ is the dispersion measure in pc cm$^{-3}$.

Only the differential delay between the two transmitted frequencies can influence waveform integrity.

For

- carrier frequency

$$
F_0=3\ \mathrm{GHz},
$$

- frequency separation

$$
2e=400\ \mathrm{kHz},
$$

- dispersion measure

$$
DM=30\ \mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}},
$$

the differential delay is approximately

$$
\Delta t_{\mathrm{DM}}
\approx
0.11\ \mathrm{ms}.
$$

Even for a symbol duration of one second,

$$
\frac{\Delta t_{\mathrm{DM}}}{T_{\mathrm{symbol}}}
\approx
10^{-4},
$$

which is negligible.

Thus, plasma dispersion introduces only a small common delay and does not distort the transmitted binary frequency sequence.

---

## 10.3 Spectral Broadening

Small-scale electron-density fluctuations produce multipath scattering that broadens extremely narrow spectral lines.

For typical Galactic sightlines near 1 GHz,

$$
\Delta f_{\mathrm{ISM}}
\lesssim
0.1\ \mathrm{Hz},
$$

while only exceptional directions produce significantly larger broadening.

At higher microwave frequencies the effect decreases rapidly.

For the proposed waveform,

$$
2e = 400\ \mathrm{kHz},
$$

therefore

$$
\frac{\Delta f_{\mathrm{ISM}}}{2e}
<
3\times10^{-7}.
$$

The expected propagation-induced broadening is more than six orders of magnitude smaller than the waveform frequency separation.

Consequently, interstellar scattering cannot merge or obscure the two transmitted frequency states.

---

## 10.4 Interstellar Scintillation

The dominant propagation impairment is intensity scintillation.

Constructive and destructive interference between multiple propagation paths causes the received power to fluctuate,

$$
P_r(t) = G(t)P_t,
$$

where $G(t)$ is a stochastic propagation gain.

Scintillation may temporarily reduce the received signal below the detection threshold or significantly enhance it.

This phenomenon affects detection probability but does not modify the underlying frequency geometry.

For nearby Galactic sources, the scintillation decorrelation bandwidth is typically much larger than several hundred kilohertz.

Therefore,

$$
2e
\ll
\Delta\nu_d,
$$

and both transmitted frequency states experience nearly identical propagation gain.

The entire waveform is simultaneously amplified or attenuated while preserving its internal spectral structure.

---

## 10.5 Phase Stability

Propagation through turbulent plasma also introduces random phase fluctuations,

$$
\phi_{\mathrm{ISM}}(t,f),
$$

which limit the achievable coherent integration time.

This limitation applies equally to conventional narrowband continuous-wave searches and to the proposed waveform.

Long observations therefore require either

- coherent integration over finite intervals followed by incoherent accumulation, or
- continuous frequency tracking during integration.

The hierarchical acquisition strategy proposed in this work performs energy detection prior to waveform decoding and is therefore naturally compatible with this propagation model.

---

## 10.6 Practical Impact

For carrier frequencies

$$
F_0
\sim
1\text{--}10\ \mathrm{GHz},
$$

and frequency-state separations

$$
2e
\sim
10^5\ \mathrm{Hz},
$$

the expected propagation effects are summarized below.

| Effect | Typical magnitude | Impact on waveform |
|---------|------------------:|-------------------|
| Free-space attenuation | Very large | Reduces SNR only |
| Plasma dispersion | <1 ms differential delay | Negligible |
| Spectral broadening | <0.1 Hz | Negligible |
| Phase fluctuations | Moderate | Limits coherent integration |
| Intensity scintillation | Significant | Alters detectability but not waveform geometry |

Thus, for typical propagation paths within approximately 1000 light-years and excluding unusually dense ionized regions, the defining frequency geometry of the proposed waveform is expected to remain essentially unchanged during transmission.

The principal propagation limitation is therefore reduced detection probability due to scintillation rather than corruption of the transmitted information.

## 10.7 Engineering Implication

The proposed waveform was intentionally designed around frequency relationships rather than absolute phase or amplitude.

Interstellar propagation may alter the received signal amplitude, introduce random phase fluctuations, and temporarily reduce the signal-to-noise ratio. However, under typical microwave propagation conditions these effects do not alter the relative positions of the two transmitted frequency states.

Therefore, for carrier frequencies of several gigahertz and frequency-state separations of several hundred kilohertz, interstellar propagation modifies signal amplitude and phase but preserves the underlying two-frequency geometry.

As a consequence, the proposed hierarchical acquisition strategy does not require explicit compensation for interstellar propagation effects prior to waveform detection. Standard corrections for Doppler drift and receiver frequency stability remain sufficient, while propagation-induced distortions primarily influence detection sensitivity rather than waveform integrity.
